Quick answer

The Cole and Mason Granite Mortar and Pestle is our top pick for its ideal balance of size, weight, and durability at a price most home cooks will find reasonable.

Go with the Jamie Oliver Signature if you want a larger, heavier set for serious pounding; choose the Amazon Basics for a no-frills bargain; pick the Frieling if you prefer non-porous stainless steel; and consider the Cilio Marble if you want a beautiful countertop piece that handles light work well.

A good mortar and pestle is one of the most versatile tools in the kitchen. It does what blenders and spice grinders can’t: let you control texture by feel, crush spices without overheating them, and turn garlic, herbs, or nuts into pastes or powders with a rhythm that feels almost meditative. The material matters most, granite is the standard for toughness and weight, marble looks elegant but can chip and absorb odors, and stainless steel is non-porous and easy to clean but often too slick for good grip. When choosing, consider the bowl’s depth and weight (a heavy base stays put), the interior finish (rough enough to grip but not so rough it’s hard to clean), and the pestle length (it should sit comfortably in your hand). Your typical batch size also guides you: a small bowl does for a single clove or a teaspoon of cumin, while a large one handles a cup of pesto or a full spice blend. Our pick: Jamie Oliver Signature Granite Mortar and Pestle.

Best for pesto and wet pastes

Stainless steel’s non-porous surface makes the Frieling Stainless Steel Mortar and Pestle the top choice for wet ingredients like basil, garlic, olive oil, and pine nuts. While you won’t get the same friction for grinding seeds, the smooth finish is actually an advantage for emulsifying pesto into a creamy paste. If you prefer a stone texture, the Cole and Mason still works well but will need hand-washing and careful drying. Our pick: Frieling Stainless Steel Mortar and Pestle.

The finish isn’t as refined as pricier options, but for someone who grinds a teaspoon of cumin a few times a week, it’s more than adequate.
