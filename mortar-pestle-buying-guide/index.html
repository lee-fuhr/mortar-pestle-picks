How to choose a mortar and pestle

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best mortar and pestle for your kitchen comes down to material, size, and your typical cooking tasks. Granite or marble sets offer durability and weight for most home cooks, while a medium capacity of about two cups strikes a practical balance between versatility and storage.

What size mortar and pestle should you buy?

Size is the first practical decision because it directly affects what you can grind comfortably. A very small mortar, often called a “spice mortar,” holds just a few tablespoons and is fine for crushing single spices or garlic. But if you regularly make pastes like pesto, curry pastes, or guacamole, you’ll want a larger bowl that gives you room to work without ingredients spilling over. A medium mortar with a capacity around two cups is the sweet spot for most households. It handles daily spice grinding, small herb chopping, and even modest batches of wet pastes. Larger four-cup mortars are great if you cook for a crowd or make big batches of pesto, but they take up more counter and storage space. Think about what you’ll actually grind most often and choose a size that fits both your recipes and your kitchen real estate.

Which material is best for your kitchen?

Granite is by far the most common and practical choice. It’s hard, heavy, and porous enough to grip spices without absorbing too much odor or staining if cleaned promptly. A solid granite set is stable during grinding and relatively easy to maintain. Marble is similar but slightly softer and more prone to scratching from abrasive spices; it’s also heavier and often more expensive. Both are excellent for dry and wet grinding. Ceramic mortars are lighter and non-porous, so they don’t absorb flavors, but they can be brittle and slippery, food tends to slide around rather than get crushed. Wooden mortars (often called “molcajetes” in Mexican cooking) are great for certain tasks like making guacamole, but they require seasoning and are not ideal for hard spices because the wood can splinter. Stainless steel or brass mortars exist but are rare; they are extremely durable but heavy and expensive, and metal pestles can feel harsh on the wrist.

Flat vs. deep bowl: what’s the difference?

The shape of the bowl directly affects your grinding technique. A flat, wide bowl, like those found in traditional Mexican molcajetes, allows you to use a rocking motion with the pestle, which is excellent for crushing wet ingredients and making pastes. The wide opening also makes it easier to see what you’re doing and scrape down the sides. Deep, narrow bowls (often called “Thai-style” mortars) are better for pounding and grinding dry spices. The steep sides keep ingredients contained, so you can apply more force without spillage. They are less suited for wet pastes because ingredients tend to climb up the walls. If you primarily grind spices, a deep bowl is efficient; if you make a lot of sauces or salsas, a shallower, wider bowl is more practical.

How to care for your mortar and pestle

Seasoning a new stone mortar is essential to remove loose grit. Most granite or marble sets benefit from a few rounds of grinding a handful of uncooked rice with water, then discarding the paste and rinsing. You’ll notice the water runs grayish at first; keep grinding until it runs clear. This also helps smooth any sharp edges. After use, rinse with hot water and scrub gently with a stiff brush, never use soap on porous stone, as it can leave a residue that affects flavor. For stubborn stains, grind a little more rice or salt. Dry thoroughly before storing. Wooden mortars should be oiled occasionally with food-grade mineral oil. Avoid putting any mortar in the dishwasher; the heat and detergent can damage the material.

What can you grind in a mortar and pestle?

A mortar and pestle is ideal for whole spices (coriander, cumin, peppercorns), fresh herbs (basil, cilantro, mint), garlic and ginger, nuts, seeds, and even small amounts of grains or salt. It excels at tasks where you want a textured, uneven grind rather than a fine powder, think crushed fennel seeds in a sausage recipe or a coarse peppercorn blend. For wet pastes like pesto or chimichurri, the mortar produces a more robust flavor than a food processor because the crushing action releases oils and juices gradually. You can also use it to make small-batch guacamole, curry pastes, or even grind dried chilies. However, it is not efficient for large quantities of very hard ingredients (like whole nutmeg or long pepper) unless you have a very heavy granite set and patience.

Frequently asked questions

Is granite mortar safe for food?

Yes, granite is naturally non-toxic and safe for food contact. Just be sure to season a new mortar to remove any loose grit. Avoid using soap on porous stone.

Can I use a mortar and pestle for wet ingredients like pesto?

Absolutely. A wide, shallow mortar is ideal for wet pastes. The grinding action releases more flavor than a food processor. Just be aware that wet ingredients can splash, so work slowly and use a pestle with a comfortable grip.

How do I season a new mortar and pestle?

Grind a few tablespoons of uncooked rice with a little water until the paste becomes white, then discard. Repeat once or twice. Rinse thoroughly and let dry. This removes stone dust and smooths the surface.

Can I put a mortar and pestle in the dishwasher?

No. The high heat and harsh detergents can damage stone, crack ceramic, or warp wood. Hand wash with hot water and a stiff brush, then dry completely.

What’s the difference between a mortar and pestle and a spice grinder?

A mortar and pestle gives you control over texture and doesn’t generate heat, preserving volatile oils. An electric spice grinder is faster and makes fine powder, but it can overheat spices and change their flavor. Mortars are better for small batches and coarse grinds.

Do I need a large or small mortar?

If you mainly grind individual spices or garlic, a small mortar (about 1 to 1.5 cups) is sufficient. For pastes, sauces, or frequent multi-spice grinds, a medium (2–3 cup) mortar is more practical. Large mortars are bulky and only needed for substantial batches.

In shortChoosing a mortar and pestle doesn’t have to be complicated. Focus on a durable material like granite, pick a size that fits your daily cooking, and consider whether you’ll grind more dry spices or wet pastes. A well-made set can last a lifetime with simple care, and it will reward you with fresher, more aromatic flavors in every dish.
