Mortar and pestle questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A mortar and pestle is a timeless kitchen tool for grinding and crushing spices, herbs, and other ingredients. The best choice depends on what you cook most: heavy granite or marble for oily spices, ceramic for easy cleaning, or wood for gentle grinding. Look for a rough interior surface for grip and a weight that feels stable in your hand.

What size mortar and pestle should I buy?

Mortar and pestles come in a range of sizes, from tiny personal versions meant for a single pinch of spice to hefty bowls big enough for a batch of pesto. A medium size, roughly two to three cups in capacity, is the most versatile for a home cook: large enough to grind a tablespoon of cumin seeds or a few garlic cloves, yet compact enough to store easily. Weight matters more than diameter. A heavier mortar stays put on the counter while you work, so look for one that feels substantial in hand. For single servings, a smaller, lighter mortar works fine, but if you often make curry pastes or nut butters, a larger, sturdier model will save you frustration.

Which material is best: granite, marble, ceramic, or wood?

Granite is the workhorse of mortars. It is dense, heavy, and has a naturally rough interior that grips seeds and spices well. The porous surface can trap flavors over time, which some cooks love for developing a seasoned patina, but you should avoid using soap on it. Marble is non-porous and stays cool, making it excellent for grinding chocolate or nuts without melting them. The downside: polished marble interiors can be slippery, so spices may slide instead of crush. Ceramic mortars are non-porous, easy to clean, and often come in bright colors, but they can chip if handled roughly. Wood mortars are gentle for seeds and herbs but absorb moisture and odors, so they are best reserved for dry or light-duty grinding.

How do I season a new mortar and pestle?

Seasoning is mainly for porous stone mortars like granite, lava rock, or unpolished marble. Start by grinding a tablespoon of raw white rice until it becomes a fine powder; this picks up any loose grit from the surface. Discard the rice powder and rinse the mortar. Next, grind a few cloves of garlic or a teaspoon of cumin seeds with a pinch of salt to embed a mild, aromatic flavor into the pores. Wipe clean and your mortar is ready to use. Non-porous mortars, glazed ceramic or polished marble, don’t need seasoning; just wash them with mild soap and water before first use.

What can I use a mortar and pestle for?

A mortar and pestle excels at tasks that benefit from a controlled, crushing motion: grinding whole spices like coriander, cumin, and peppercorns; smashing garlic and ginger into a paste; making herb rubs, pestos, and guacamole. It also works beautifully for grinding seeds like sesame or flax, and for crushing toasted nuts. It is less efficient for large-volume wet grinding, say, a cup of wet curry paste, because the bowl is small and the process is manual. For high-volume tasks, a blender or food processor is faster. The real strength of a mortar and pestle is the aromatic oils it releases and the texture control you get with every press and twist.

How do I clean and care for my mortar and pestle?

For porous stone mortars (granite, unglazed lava), never use soap, it can seep into the pores and leave a chemical taste. Instead, scrub the bowl with coarse salt and a stiff brush, then rinse with water and dry thoroughly. A paste of baking soda and water helps remove stubborn stains or odors. For non-porous mortars (polished marble, glazed ceramic, glass), mild dish soap and a sponge are fine. Always dry completely to prevent water spots or mold. Avoid soaking any mortar for long periods, and never put stone or ceramic mortars in the dishwasher.

What to look for in a quality mortar and pestle?

The most important feature is a rough, unpolished interior surface. A slick interior will let seeds slide around instead of grinding them. The mortar should be heavy enough to stay put, a lightweight plastic or thin stone bowl will skid across your counter. The pestle should fit comfortably in your hand and reach the bottom of the bowl without much strain. Check for a flat or slightly rounded base so the mortar doesn’t wobble. Avoid models with very shallow bowls, as ingredients tend to spill out. A well-made mortar feels balanced and solid; the price usually reflects the quality of the stone and the finishing work.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to season a mortar and pestle?

Only if your mortar is made from a porous stone such as granite, lava rock, or unpolished marble. Seasoning removes loose grit and embeds a light flavor layer. Non-porous mortars (glazed ceramic, polished marble, glass) just need a wash before first use.

Can I use a mortar and pestle for grinding coffee?

Yes, but you will get an uneven grind, perfect for Turkish coffee or coarse grounds, but not ideal for drip coffee. If you want consistent fine espresso powder, a burr grinder is much more reliable.

Is a marble mortar and pestle better than granite?

It depends on your use. Marble stays cool and non-porous, which is great for grinding chocolate or nuts without them turning oily. Granite is heavier and has a naturally rough interior that grips spices better, making it a top choice for spice grinding.

How do I remove strong odors from a mortar and pestle?

Grind raw white rice or stale bread in the mortar to absorb smells, then rinse. For persistent odors, grind a tablespoon of baking soda or fresh lemon zest, then wash as usual. Avoid soap on porous stone.

Can I use a mortar and pestle for wet ingredients like garlic and ginger?

Absolutely. A rough-surfaced mortar is ideal for breaking down fibrous garlic, ginger, or lemongrass into smooth pastes. The crushing action releases more essential oils than a knife or press.

What is the difference between a mortar and pestle and a spice grinder?

A mortar and pestle gives you full control over texture, from coarse cracks to fine powders, and releases more aromatic oils because of the crushing action. An electric spice grinder is faster and more consistent for dry spices but can heat up, reducing some volatile flavors.

In shortChoosing the right mortar and pestle comes down to matching the material and size to your cooking style. Treat it well, and it will last for years, delivering fresh flavor that no electric gadget can match.
